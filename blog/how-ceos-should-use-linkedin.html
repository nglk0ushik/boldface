How CEOs Should Use LinkedIn

May 31, 20267 min readBoldface

The CEO problem on LinkedIn

Most CEOs use LinkedIn in one of two broken ways. They either ignore it entirely, leaving a stale profile that reads like a job description, or they treat it as a corporate broadcast channel, resharing company news with a line of bland commentary on top. Neither builds anything. The first wastes the single most valuable platform for a senior leader to be known on. The second actively signals that there is no real person behind the title.

What a CEO presence is actually for

A CEO is not trying to go viral. The job of a CEO's LinkedIn is narrower and more valuable. It is to make sure that when an investor, a senior hire, a journalist or a major customer looks you up, they find someone who clearly knows what they are talking about and has a point of view worth respecting. That is the entire goal. Everything else is noise.

Write about decisions, not platitudes

The content that works for senior leaders is specific and lived. Write about the actual decisions you face, the trade-offs you weigh, the things you changed your mind about. A CEO who writes about how they think about capital allocation, or why they restructured a team, or what they got wrong in a hire, is infinitely more compelling than one who posts about leadership in the abstract. Specificity is credibility. Vagueness is the thing that makes people scroll past.

Have an actual opinion

The instinct of most senior people is to stay safe, to say the agreeable thing, to avoid taking a position. On LinkedIn this is fatal because a safe opinion is indistinguishable from no opinion. The CEOs who build real authority are willing to say what they actually believe, including the uncomfortable parts. You do not need to be combative. You need to be willing to be clear about where you stand.

How much time it really takes

The biggest objection is time, and it is a fair one. A CEO does not have hours a week for this. The good news is that they do not need them. The thinking is the scarce part, and the CEO has to supply that. The writing, structuring, scheduling and management can be handled by someone else. Most senior leaders who do this well spend under thirty minutes a week of their own time once the system is running, mostly approving and supplying raw thinking through voice notes.

The mistake to avoid

Do not delegate the voice. You can delegate the work, but the point of view has to be genuinely yours, or the whole thing collapses the moment someone meets you in person and you sound nothing like your posts. The best executive content is ghostwritten in structure and self in substance. The thinking is the CEO's. The craft is someone else's. That is the line that keeps it honest and effective.
