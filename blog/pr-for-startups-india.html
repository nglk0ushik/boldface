PR for Startups: How Founders Actually Get Media Coverage in India

May 31, 20267 min readBoldface

Most founders misunderstand how media coverage actually happens. They imagine PR as a machine. You write a press release, you send it to a list of journalists, and coverage comes out the other end. Then they try it, hear nothing back, and conclude that PR does not work for a company their size. The truth is that the machine they imagined never existed. Real media coverage in India works on relationships, relevance and timing, and once you understand that, the whole thing becomes far less mysterious and far more achievable.

Why press releases do not work

A journalist receives a flood of press releases every day, almost all of which are about companies announcing things that matter to the company and to nobody else. A funding round, a new hire, a product update. From the founder's side these feel like news. From the journalist's side they are noise, because their job is not to publicize your milestones. It is to tell stories their readers find interesting. The press release fails because it answers the wrong question. It tells the journalist what you want to say, not what their audience wants to read.

Journalists need stories, not announcements

The shift that changes everything is to stop thinking about what you want to announce and start thinking about what story you can help a journalist tell. A journalist covering your sector needs angles, data, trends and credible voices to quote. If you can be the founder who explains what is really happening in your industry, who has a sharp opinion on a shift everyone is noticing, or who has data nobody else has shared, you become useful to them. Useful sources get covered, quoted and called again. Self-promoters get ignored.

The India-specific reality

The Indian media landscape has its own texture. There are the large business publications, a deep layer of startup and sector-specific media, and a growing world of newsletters, podcasts and creators who now hold real influence. Coverage in a focused sector publication that your exact buyers read is often worth more than a passing mention in a large outlet that reaches everyone and no one. Founders chase the big names for ego, but the smaller, sharper placements frequently do more for the business. Match the outlet to the audience you actually want, not to the logo that sounds most impressive.

Build the relationship before you need it

The founders who get covered consistently do something most do not. They build relationships with journalists before they have something to pitch. They engage with their work, offer useful commentary, become a known and reliable voice in the space. So when the journalist needs a source or the founder has a genuine story, there is already a relationship to build on. Cold pitching to someone who has never heard of you is the hardest possible version of this. Warm pitching to someone who already respects you is a different game entirely.

What founders can actually do

Start by becoming visible and credible in your space, because journalists find sources the same way everyone else does, often through LinkedIn and through who is already being talked about. Develop a genuine point of view on your industry that is worth quoting. Identify the specific publications and writers who cover your world, and follow what they actually write rather than blasting everyone. And when you reach out, lead with the story and the value to their reader, not with your announcement. None of this is fast, and none of it is guaranteed, because editorial decisions are never in your control. But it is how coverage actually gets earned, and it is entirely within reach for a founder willing to do it properly.
