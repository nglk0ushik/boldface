LinkedIn Benchmarks for SaaS Founders

May 31, 20267 min readBoldface

Why benchmarks matter, and why most are useless

Every SaaS founder who starts posting eventually asks the same question. Is this working? The problem is that most benchmarks floating around are either vanity numbers from people selling a course, or numbers from creators whose whole job is content. A SaaS founder is not trying to become an influencer. They are trying to build enough credibility and reach that the right buyers, hires and investors take them seriously. The benchmarks that matter are the ones tied to that goal.

Follower growth that is actually healthy

For a SaaS founder starting from a small base and posting consistently with a clear point of view, steady monthly growth in the hundreds is a strong sign in the first six months. The exact number matters far less than the trend and the quality. A thousand followers who are operators, buyers and peers in your category is worth more than ten thousand who followed for a viral post unrelated to your work. Growth that comes from saying something specific to your audience compounds. Growth that comes from chasing broad reach rarely converts into anything useful.

Engagement, read properly

Likes are the weakest signal. Comments are better because they show someone cared enough to respond. The strongest signal of all does not appear in your analytics at all. It is the conversation that starts because someone read your post, the message in your inbox, the line in a sales call where a prospect says they have been following your writing. If you are getting those, the content is working even if the public numbers look modest.

Inbound, the number that pays the bills

For a SaaS founder, the benchmark that truly matters is inbound. Within three to six months of consistent, specific content, you should start seeing conversations you did not have to chase. A few qualified inbound conversations a month, attributable to LinkedIn, is a meaningful result for most early and growth-stage founders. It usually grows from there as the body of work accumulates and your name circulates.

A realistic timeline

The honest timeline looks like this. The first month feels like shouting into a void, and that is normal. By month two or three, engagement becomes consistent. By month four to six, inbound starts and your name begins to travel. By month nine to twelve, you are recognised in your category and conversations open warmer than they used to. Anyone promising faster is selling you something. The compounding is real, but it is not instant.

The one benchmark to ignore

Posting frequency as a goal in itself. A SaaS founder who posts twice a week with a real point of view will outperform one who posts daily with nothing to say. The benchmark is not how often you post. It is whether what you post is worth someone's attention.
