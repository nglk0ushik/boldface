What to Post on LinkedIn When You Think You Have Nothing to Say

May 31, 20266 min readBoldface

Almost every founder who decides to take LinkedIn seriously hits the same wall within a week. They sit down to write, stare at the empty box, and conclude they have nothing to say. It feels like a content problem. It is almost never a content problem. People who run companies, advise clients and make hard decisions every day are not short of things to say. They are short of permission to say the ordinary things, because they assume only the extraordinary things are worth posting. That assumption is the whole problem.

You are sitting on more than you think

The belief that you have nothing to say usually means you have decided your actual daily experience is too obvious to be interesting. The decision you made this week, the mistake you watched a client make for the third time, the thing you used to believe and no longer do. To you these feel mundane because you live inside them. To the person reading, who does not have your experience, they are exactly the insight they were looking for. What is obvious to you is valuable to someone two steps behind you, and that is most of your audience.

Start with what annoyed you

If you are stuck, the fastest way in is to notice what frustrated you recently. The advice you keep seeing that is wrong. The mistake you watch people make again and again. The thing everyone in your industry says that you quietly disagree with. Frustration is useful because it is honest and it is specific, and a post written from genuine irritation almost always has a clear point of view. You are not manufacturing an opinion. You are reporting one you already hold.

Write down the questions you actually answer

Think about the questions people ask you in real life. The thing clients always want to know. The advice you find yourself repeating in every other meeting. The questions a younger version of you would have killed to have answered. Each of those is a post, and you already know the answer cold because you have given it a dozen times. You are not inventing content. You are writing down what you already say out loud.

Tell the story, not the lesson

When founders do try to post, they often reach for grand lessons and end up sounding like everyone else. The fix is to go smaller and more specific. Instead of writing about leadership in the abstract, write about the actual moment a decision went wrong and what you did next. Specifics are what people remember and trust, because they prove you lived it. A real story from your week beats a polished principle every single time, because nobody can fake the details.

The real unlock

The blank page is not telling you that you have nothing to say. It is telling you that you are filtering too hard, throwing out the real and specific things because they feel too small, and waiting for a big idea that rarely comes. Lower the bar. Write the ordinary, specific, honest thing you actually think. That is the content people connect with, and you already have more of it than you could post in a year. The hard part was never finding something to say. It was giving yourself permission to say it plainly.
